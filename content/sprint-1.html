Choosing a domain

Sprint 1Making sense of online research space

Goal

Decide on a domain and better understand the space around that chosen domain

Deliverable


Research report synthesized from insights

The survey allowed us to get a broad understanding of the landscape of online research; the literature review was important from an academic standpoint to better understand our client’s background; and the directed storytelling was critical to start empathizing and understanding users. The research report helped us to synthesize the findings from our survey, literature review and interviews into broader insights that could be presented to the client.


From the UX Toolkit

Online survey, directed storytelling & usability studies

Process

In our first post-kickoff sprint, our project had somewhat of an unexpected start. Before having the official kickoff with the client, the most that any of our group knew about the project was what was given to us over winter break in the form of the project descriptions. Based on the prompt, our group assumed that the aim of the project was to explore sensemaking and how to accelerate cold starts in research, specifically within the domain of online shopping. In the course of the kickoff, we were told that the project didn’t necessarily need to focus on shopping as a domain, and that if we as a team thought that another area would better suit our interests (albeit still working toward the same knowledge and sensemaking goals), that we would be free to change.

Thus, the plans we had originally made exclusively focused on shopping and trying to delineate which areas within shopping would provide most fruitful were instead replaced by methods to help our team determine which domain we wanted to pursue. Our group felt that a broad survey aimed at elucidating which of these domains were most commonly looked at within online research would be helpful.

Our survey revolved around five distinct domains: shopping, travel, health & wellness, job hunting, and event planning. The survey asked people what they researched and how and where that research happened. The survey was answered by 241 people, with the largest percentage of respondents favoring shopping as the domain they did the most research (36.1%). We also found that only a small number of users were actually doing intensive research (“super-optimizers”), or creating artifacts as a part of their research, something the client was specifically interested in. Nonetheless, we were energized that our initial feelings about moving forward with shopping as a domain were validated and jumped into user interviews to learn even more about motivations behind online research.

We focused on the most common domains for online research (shopping and travel) to understand more about motivation behind online research and the journey taken. We utilized directed storytelling to gain further insights from users. After conducting 11 interviews, our team got more clarity about the use of tools online in researching, finding that more people used tools for travel purposes than shopping; users tended to use familiar tools, rather than searching for new ones (Google tools featured heavily); and that more intense purchases warranted deeper searching and artifact creation.

Based on our all findings, we synthesized the data to pull out key insights into a research report. We also leveraged the literature review completed in the previous sprint to bolster our credibility with our client, given the client team has been researching this space for over 10 years. We found some interesting insights related to three key stages of online research that we found: information gathering, synthesis and decision-making. With information gathering, we learned that people used established curation features, like “best seller” flags, to make the process of gathering information much more condensed. We also learned that during information synthesis, collaboration is much more common in travel than shopping. Finally, we learned that when people make decisions, they largely rely on reviews & ratings to make decisions quickly and the process of deciding is not a collaborative one. However, our survey had indicated that shopping was the most common online research domain. We had learned in our client kickoff that Knowledge Accelerator’s primary goal was to build a “generalist” tool. Because we wanted to build a tool that ultimately was for this “generalist”, online shopping was where we wanted to focus our time and that’s how we continued to narrow to our next key question.

We ended the sprint excited by the information we were finally starting to get about research behavior and our collective decision to remain with shopping as a research domain.

Reflection

We can use online shopping as a domain to gain insight into building a more generalist sensemaking tool.
