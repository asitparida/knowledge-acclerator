Designing from user behavior

Sprint 3Starting the ideation process

Goal

Draw specific insights from primary research and identify design opportunities in order to inform the direction of our “Design as Research” phase

Deliverable

Journey Maps + Storyboards

We created process journeys to synthesize findings from our initial round of research because these helped us create a shared understanding of the current state between our team and the client. We also drew specific pain points from each journey and used those as seeds for our first-round ideation, which were later turned into storyboards so that we can validate the needs of users and explore possible design orientations.

Process

After affinitizing findings from user interviews about online shopping behavior, we found that our previous assumption that there are different types of shoppers (e.g. Super-optimizers, information gatherers, and satisfiers) wasn’t supported by the research. It is rarely the case that a user exhibits the same pattern of researching for information when shopping. In fact, we found that the processes people go through when shopping for something vary depending on a multitude of factors -- importance/value of the purchase, prior knowledge about the domain, time available, number of viable options, etc.. Therefore, instead of shopper personas and their research style, we decided to make process journeys that capture the steps people take and the pain points they encounter during the shopping research process.

Through our collected data, we developed three archetypes that captured the majority of the processes -- iterative, linear, and condensed.

The linear model is a straightforward process characterized by a single round of research, followed by contextualization (how does this product fit into my life), then a decision & potential subsequent purchase.

The iterative process is a bit more complicated: information gathering and contextualization happens more than once in a looping pattern. Eventually this leads to a decision and purchase, but this process may yet be repeated again if the purchase turns out to be unsatisfactory.

Download

There was also a shorter, condensed model identified: the user goes into shopping process already having contextualization, which abbreviates the process.

We identified some factors for why people would exhibit one behavior or another when shopping, which include (but aren’t limited to): the importance or value of the purchase; prior knowledge about the domain; time available; and number of viable options.

These behavior models and their associated pain points helped to identify some general design opportunities going into our ideation phase, which included: collapsing the foraging and contextualization process (i.e. allowing people to see both more information about a product as well as how it could fit into their specific situation simultaneously, instead of sequentially); providing indication of the research progress; and better translating the intent into a specific search.

We selected five broad concepts to test with users in order to validate if the pain points we identified and possible solution directions resonated, using storyboards to test our hypotheses with representative users.We found that in general, people expected a tool that helps with both information gathering and decision making, rather than one that helps with only one or the other. More specific insights include:

  • People want others’ opinions, but need to know more about their identity in order to validate the authenticity of the information.
  • People want to have agency in their shopping decision making process.
  • People found visual solutions to be more appealing.

Although some aspects of the design ideas resonated with the participants, none of them seemed to stand out as absolutely indispensable. Regardless, they were informative and helped us scaffold our design ideas going forward. We took these findings and used them to hone our ideation process to further discover and validate user needs.

Reflection

There are pain points that can be observed across all shopping behavior models, and people expect a tool that helps with both information gathering and decision making, rather than one that helps with only one or the other.
